About

In the Spring of 2017 I noticed that my Mechanics class, despite homework practice and having used it in previous classes, was struggling some with basic concepts and everyday usage of Python. It felt like they were proficient in the narrow applications of what they’d done in class, but suddenly were in deep water if doing even basic things outside their comfort zone. I made Digital Dodgeball as a tentative idea in how to address that issue in an outside-of-the-classroom and fun fashion.

Because our physics department has focused heavily on Python for our classroom (and research) needs, I wanted everything to be written in Python for direct applicability to students needs. And because many students have limited time to work or play around with something outside of class, I wanted the game to be immediately tactile and approachable. For these reasons, after experimenting around with what online offerings I could find, I decided my best route would just be to make something myself. And it was decidedly helped by my desire to try my hand at making (and learning how to make) something like this in the first place.

And so after much mucking around, debugging, and fun, Digital Dodgeball came into existence.
